A Holocene Holiday - A short story

‘Don’t you fucking do it, Eddie!’ I said as the countdown initiated. I saw he was going to do it. I decided to change tack and smiled, splaying my hands in a gesture of placation. ‘Surely we can talk about this?’

Standing on the correct side of the escape pod hatch, Captain Eddie didn’t smile. I knew I probably should have listened to the lizard. ‘Don’t go into the escape pod; Eddie’s going to blow you out of the hatch,’ Bob the Lizard had said, but how was I to decipher such cryptic warnings?

I saw the captain reach up towards an unseen button. I pursed my lips with mild concern. Well, shit, I thought as the alacritous cannibal engaged the release mechanism. My infinitely witty and cutting scathe was interrupted ferociously as I was ejected from The Trestle. I smacked my head into the pod’s porthole and lost consciousness.

You might have noticed that I skipped some of my story; well, tough, because I’m not going to recap the whole thing, as I may be free of this cell soon. You have the gist of the tale though, I’m sure; I’m Adam Ash, the galaxy’s greatest, humblest space detective, yada yada, so on, so forth. I’m sure you remember all the mishaps and miscreants I’ve encountered thus far; Majestic Rex the murderously effusive robot, Seabiscuit the singing corpse frog, and now Captain Eddie and his mission to taste the flesh of every species in the galaxy. Remember? No? Well, sometimes I feel people just don’t want to listen to my riveting stories. What? You don’t want to listen to my story?

Well, anyway, I was shooting through the cold void of the Outer Boundaries in that spinning tumble drier for God knows how long as I intermittently regained and lost my

senses. Eventually, the escape pod’s tumultuous spinning eased, and I no longer had to battle consciousness shooting out of my nose and mouth and ears like leaking jet fuel. After the dizziness subsided, I pressed my face to the porthole, certain that there was some way out of this.

It’s quite beautiful up there, you know, beyond the exosphere. Have you ever been? Even before I left Earth, it always fascinated me, that unending visage of coal-dark oil and its thousand-eyed stare. It’s why I was so excited to leave Earth, and Titan, I suppose. Things have a way of falling short of expectations; hell, you certainly fell short of my expectations. But the first time I skirted the Pillars of Creation on a battered plasma-skip or felt a warp engine rattle and rearrange my very atoms, there’s nothing that can compare. So many things to see in this little home cluster of ours, isn’t there? Things to do, people to meet and befriend and …

Sorry, where was I? Ah, yes, the tomshittery with Captain Eddie!

I fucked up; I knew that Captain Eddie had been up to something terrible, but I hadn’t known that the captain knew that I knew he was up to something terrible, you know? Though my plans went awry, at least this way my greatest fear had been averted; being eaten alive.1 My musings on cannibalism were interrupted by flashing amber and the beeping that I had become far too familiar with in the past year. An incongruently calm voice said, ‘Planetary collision imminent,’ and here I am on your strange planet!

When I emerged from the pod, I was ill at ease – the jungles here, the birds, the bees, those asshole cicadas, it was all unnervingly similar to Earth’s biome. I’d been lucky not to

teeter over the plateau where the pod had crashed, but it did give me a glorious view of the land, rolling hills like the carved scapula of a dead giant covered in follicles of tree ferns.2

There seems to be a highly strange confluence throughout the galaxy wherein planets seem to be possessed of a singularly ridiculous theme; Moribund is Gothic, Auboroboro is Blade Runner, and Planet Rat has all those gorillas. Your planet is no different. From that lovely plateau I could see some buildings in the valley below. So, with the pluck of adventure feathering my cap, or rather my cowboy hat in this case, I set on my way. It wasn’t long before I came across the park.

It had seen better days. The thoroughfare was littered with the carcasses of palm trees and the fallen straw gables of concrete edifices. Half of the streetlamps had collapsed before the plethora of arcades and gift shops, barring any easy passage, and the rollercoaster track that circumvented the park had fallen near the visitor centre, terminating like an unfinished thought.3 The architecture itself was odd; it seemed to be of familiar human design, consisting of concrete and white limestone, but built on a far larger scale than anything I’d ever seen.

I thought it best to set up camp and search for any signs of life in the morning; I took up short residence in one of the gift shops, this one titled Holocene Holiday Shopping. It was a sad looking thing with its shattered windows and half-collapsed roof. It didn’t offer much protection from the elements, but it provided more shelter than its fallen brethren strewn about the place.

I entered and perused the shop’s displays, and my confusion grew. On the shelves were battalions of stuffed toys crafted to resemble naked men and women of all hues, their

heads and hands and feet portrayed in bulbous, cartoonish proportions. Then there were the lunchboxes, with more naked men and women depicted on them, many of them running through tropical fields and lazing beside crystalline waterfalls. What sort of resort I had happened upon? I was reminded of the slave colonies of the Outer Boundaries, where malignant aliens would establish hunting compounds populated with intelligent species from conquered worlds to be released and killed and eaten; had I landed on one of these hunting complexes?4 Disconcerted but resolute as ever that I would not be murdered and devoured by aliens, I went to sleep.

I awoke to a strange, earthen smell, like the robust odour of a dirty stable. In the grey morning gloom, I saw a man standing outside of the gift shop. Rather, I saw his torso; he was stupendously tall, his head hidden from sight. Glad to see another human, I rushed to speak with him, hoping to learn where the heck I was.

Well, it turns out he wasn’t human, not fully anyway. He was a skittish fellow; I exited the shop and there was the clip clopping of hooves echoing on asphalt as he tore off. It took me a moment to realise that he was not, in fact, a horse rider; he was the horse. I’d never seen a centaur before. Hell, I didn’t know they were real. I noticed that the horseman had written a message before the gift shop, the letters sloping and elegant: BEWARE THE LIZARDS. Perturbed that the alien centaur knew English, I continued my journey.

In the distance there seemed to be a radio tower, its warning lights blinking sluggishly as dusk dusked once again. Off I went in the hopes of sending an SOS.

I was struggling valiantly through a thicket when I heard voices. Crouching low and moving with painstaking care, I moved through the brush, grimacing as the ferns about me rustled. As the voices grew louder, I paused and noticed a set of footprints in the dirt, three-toed tracks that seemed to terminate in stubby little claws. I crept closer to the chittering conversation. I had just reached the edge of the thicket when the voices became intelligible. Hidden, I listened.

‘We need to find that fucking monstrosity before it kills anymore of our researchers,’ one of the voices whined, a strange huffing between each breath. ‘Dr Wong is furious that the park has been out of commission as long as it has!’ I grimaced; this was not the first time I’d been called a monster, and I didn’t even remember killing any researchers since I’d arrived, though it was quite possible that I’d Dorothy’d someone when I landed.

‘Calm down, Clemens,’ said another voice, softer but still accentuated by the strange huffing. ‘Once we re-establish power to the towers and the surveillance stations it will be a matter of simplicity to find it. Don’t worry your little bald head.’

‘Hmph, I don’t know, Jackie,’ Clemens said. ‘Dr Wong said if we can’t capture it by the end of the month the investors will pull out of the project. Jesus Christ, the investors don’t even know about the deaths in the park yet!’ I frowned. Jesus Christ? Had the Christian fleets already thrust this far into the Boundaries? I sidled closer to get a better look at the suspiciously human-sounding aliens.

‘Clemens, really, you’re going to make me pull out my—wait, did you hear that?’ I froze. There came a guttural snorting such as that of a frightened horse shying from a rattling rattlesnake. ‘Whoever’s out there, we’re armed! You’d better show yourself or we’ll … we’ll …’

‘Jackie, I can see him! I can see his cowboy hat!’ Clemens said excitedly. I heard the distinct racking of a shotgun.

‘Show yourself!’

I hissed a quiet curse but didn’t budge. ‘I’ll shoot!’ Jackie said, and her voice sounded much closer, though I still couldn’t see her through the brush.

‘Wait! Wait, don’t shoot!’ I called. A hero always knows when to surrender. ‘I’m coming out!’

‘You’d better!’

‘Jackie, it’s not the—’

‘Shut up, Clemens!’

‘Before I give myself up,’ I said, ‘I need assurance that you’re not going to eat me.’

There was a pause. A disconcertingly long pause.

‘Why the fuck would we eat you?’

‘I dunno,’ I mumbled, suddenly shy. ‘Lots of people have tried to eat me before.’ Why wouldn’t they want to eat me? There’s no doubt in my mind that I’m delicious.

‘We’re not going to eat you, dumbass. Now get out of those bushes, this is private property.’

I removed myself from the brush, hands raised, eyes closed lest I saw the muzzle flash of doom. When I stepped to, there was a prolonged silence, and I thought maybe I’d scared them away. Expecting more centaurs, I opened my eyes and beheld an oddity.

At the foot of the radio tower stood two dinosaurs in lab coats. Specifically, they were two pachycephalosaurs, the rondure of their skulls sheening in the sunlight. One held a clipboard in its stubby little hands, its mottled tail of forest green swishing nervously behind it as I stood there, dumbfounded. The other, its beady eyes wide with surprise, held a teeny tiny shotgun that was now levelled at my chest.

The tension snapped.

‘What the fuck?’ we three said in unison.

‘A talking dinosaur!’ I said delightedly, for I truly loved dinosaurs.

‘A talking human!’ the clipboard-wielding pachy said in alarm.

‘This is … well, this is just swell,’ I said, a tear of joy coming to my eye.5 I took another step forward to hug these corporeal childhood dreams.

‘Clemens, steal his fucking blood!’ the dinosaur with the shotgun squealed as it raised its armament skyward and blasted buckshot into the air.

I flinched as the other pachy threw his clipboard to the ground and charged at me, his head levelled, his body transmuted into a keratinous battering ram. The little fucker was quicker than he had any right to be, and before I knew it, I was thrust back into the thicket. There was a great deal of thrashing and punching and kicking as we struggled. I lay prostrate as he repeatedly headbutted me.

‘Mercy!’ I yielded heroically, and to my surprise the pachy stopped ramming his bowling ball head into my gut.

‘Jackie, should I kill him? It’d be easier to steal his blood if I killed him.’

‘But we can steal more blood if we keep him alive. Hold on.’

A radio brrrped nearby. ‘Dr Wong, this is Jackie. We’ve made an unusual discovery …’ Her voice faded into obscurity as I tried to clutch everything that hurt.

Some time passed, I’m not sure how much, when Jackie’s voice loomed in my ear. ‘Dr Wong wants to see you, human. Welcome to Holocene Park.’

‘So, here I am,’ Adam said. He sat in an eggshell blue cell enclosed by spotless steel bars, his tattered cowboy hat beside him on the bench. His other hand was slipped through the bars and, in a tendon-ripping death grip, clutched the rough-skinned arm of a petite microceratops. The tiny, blue-hued dinosaur lay on the ground, its crest-shaped head shaking as it struggled through fatigue. A trolley bearing water bottles stood beside it.

‘It’s been … it’s been eighteen hours,’ the micro said. ‘I just asked if you wanted water, and you gave me … you gave me your whole life story.’

‘Oh, my bad,’ Adam said and released the microceratops. He frowned as the micro tried to climb to its feet, wavered, then collapsed. ‘You should probably drink some water or eat or something,’ Adam said, shaking his head.

‘I can see God,’ the micro said as its eyes fluttered shut and lay its head upon the ground.

Nearby a door shnkked open. Adam stood as the fluorescent lights were eclipsed and a shadow enveloped him. He looked up, saw the dim outline of rippling musculature under an ill-fitting lab coat, tiny, forked arms, the curvature of a crushing jaw, two amber eyes.

‘Wowzers,’ Adam said as the tyrant lizard looked down on him. ‘Dr Wong, I assume?’

The tyrannosaur grunted, and there was the scrabbling of small claws along the waxed floors. Adam looked downward as a tiny green dinosaur, no longer than his forearm, darted between the T-rex’s legs. This dinosaur also wore a lab coat, and a pair of bucket glasses.

‘Hubert, please see that Phillips is given some water and rest,’ the little dinosaur said. The T-rex nodded and stooped, delicately picking the microceratops up with its jaws. The colossus ambled away through a large door, its footsteps resonating in Adam’s chest cavity.

‘Just like in the movie,’ Adam said, smiling as he pointed at the T-rex.

‘Assuredly,’ the procompsognathus said. ‘I’m Dr Wong.’

‘I’m Adam Ash,’ said Adam Ash, studying the doctor. ‘I’ve a lot of questions.’

Wong waved a hand impatiently. ‘I listened as you put Phillips into a coma. The long and short of it is that two distant solar systems bore congruent biological life cycles, one human, one dinosaur. It seems that our histories are nearly identical, except for the fact that your species made a film about cloning dinosaurs.’ He pushed his glasses up his snout with a slender claw and clutched the clipboard tight to his chest. ‘I, however, will make cloning humans a reality.’

Adam nodded. ‘Okay, so you’re going to clone me? I had a clone before, I’d always wanted one, but it didn’t end well; I kept forgetting to feed the dumb fucker. I’m sure my spawn will be safe with you. Can I go now?’

‘I’m afraid not,’ Wong said, hopping up and down. Adam couldn’t tell if it was out of excitement or frustration or if it was just a weird dinosaur thing. ‘You see, we’ve had a problem with the genome sequencing of extinct humans as we could never find a complete

strand of human DNA; bones calcify and the genetic material leeches away, gone forever. So, we had to use—'

‘Donkey DNA,’ Adam said, thinking of the centaur.

‘Horse DNA,’ Wong snapped. ‘We had to use mammalian DNA to complete the mammalian sequence, it’s the only thing that made sense. We were successful only once, and it birthed a monstrous crossbreed. We call him Bobby Horselegs; he has the intellectual capacity of a super genius and the horsepower of approximately one-half horse. We couldn’t restrain him, and this is my shame to bear. He’s been killing off my staff one by one – even Hubert can’t find the goddamned aberration and kill him.’

Adam sniffled glumly, knowing that the little dinosaur was going to force him to find the half-human, half-horse monster.

‘I want you to find the half-human, half-horse monster, Adam Ash,’ Wong said gravely. ‘I want you to save my life’s work – Holocene Park cannot fail.’

‘I’m, uh, not good at finding monsters.’

‘You said it yourself – Majestic Rex the murderous robot, Seabiscuit the corpse frog. You’ll do a sterling job.’ Hubert re-entered the room as Wong spoke. The T-rex glared at Adam as Wong unlocked the cell door. ‘Off you go now,’ Wong said. ‘Work as if you’re life depended on it!’

I fucking hate dinosaurs, Adam thought as Hubert escorted him out of the jail.
