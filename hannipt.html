The Battle of Hannipt - An exerpt from the novel, Gildayne

Battle of Hannipt


Day 301



Lying quietly amongst dead leaves and dead men, William DeLat listened to the shattering of a dying world.


They never reached Fort Lunonen. Still on the western banks of the Demerero River and less than a day from their destination, they had received the word; the Calmodians were advancing.


The order from General Kerghast, safe in his quarters on an offshore destroyer, had swept through the ranks; the men of the Tenth Division, joined by remnants of the Fourth Army, were to reconnoitre with a contingent of the Royal Syspirron Cavalry and occupy the territory west of the Demerero. They were to stop Cal from taking the river crossing to the fort.


Sunlit gloom and the onslaught of muggy air diffused through the rainforest. The col trees about him shivered tremulously as artillery roared in the distance, though the towering guns were many miles away.


Leaves fell gracefully through the air, alighting on William. He didn’t brush them off, for fear that his movements would be seen. Gunfire popped incessantly in the distance, like torrential rain smacking a tin roof in great droves. William couldn’t hear the screams, but he knew they were there, drowned out by the barrages. His sweaty hands slipped on his rifle, a filthy ’18 Lantus Bolter. Careful now, the safety is off, he thought to himself, wiping his right hand on his dark brown uniform. A frightfully potent mix of fear and humidity filled the air.


A Gildaynean Remora zipped overheard, its sleek hull glinting through the canopy, its propellers whining as it disappeared.


William lay in the well of a looming col tree, its roots splayed out like great webbed toes. His rifle rested on a cleft in the bark; he had a clear line of sight of the glade ahead. The edge of the glade was only about seven hundred feet before him, but from east to west it was five miles wide. Twelve battalions of the Fourth Army, twelve thousand men now bolstered by the Tenth Division, lay in wait.


To his right, at the tip of the clearing, a knobbly hill rose and swelled to the east, disappearing behind the col trees. Somewhere beyond, the hill dipped and terminated in a sheer cliff, the Demerero River coiling about its base. To William’s left, propped against the deepest recesses of the col’s roots, was Renn Vigast, chewing a col leaf that he had snatched from the air.


‘This will go splendidly,’ Renn, a petty noble from Austglen, whispered. The Calmodians had made a probing attack here the day previous. Their foe had waited patiently, testing the glade’s fortifications with methodical intent whilst their air force abraded the Gildaynean lines. They had driven the Gildayneans back from the glade, though they had sustained substantial casualties themselves and had retreated, unable to maintain the position.


The rest of the company murmured as they lay in foxholes or behind pocked sandbags. Autocannon teams waited, hidden in brambles and clefts in the soil. A scribbled line of Gildaynean carcasses wound through the trees, disappearing over the undulations of Hannipt’s bed. Most were in foxholes, but many had been caught out of cover, slung corpses shredded by a hail of Calmodian steel.


William could not help but stare at the bodies strewn about him.


‘Shut up and stop hiding in the shade,’ William whispered back to Renn, glancing nervously at the steep hill that descended into the glade. He held his rifle steady. A hand fell on William’s shoulder. It was Tamper.


‘Steady?’ the sergeant asked.


‘Steady.’


William cast a glance to the east where the hill rose. Privates Amon and Gregory crouched in a ditch crested by sandbags to his right; beyond them, Robert and Lou lay prone under the wide leaves of a berrybush.


‘Sergeant, are you and your men ready for this? Remoras say Cal’s on the way, be here any minute now.’


William looked over his shoulder and saw that Captain Cobbler had crawled through the foliage. Tamper nodded. William noticed, for the first time, that a dead radioman lay next to Tamper. He held his radio in his lap; the device had been struck by some explosive munition that had punched a hole clean through, its mechanical guts mingling with the innards of its operator. The radioman, no more than a youth, reminded William of James Weaver.


‘They’re ready, captain,’ Tamper said, his steel helmet low over his face. He was calm. Tamper didn’t tear his eyes away from the iron sights of his Lantus autorifle, its compact wooden frame tucked firmly into his shoulder, the barrel short and ribbed.


‘Fucking fantastic,’ Cobbler said, his white teeth stark against his muddied face. ‘Remember, wait for them to commit to the glade before you open fire. Keep them pinned. The Syspirron will take the flank and rear. You need to be ready to move if Cal pushes up the hill.’


Cobbler was about to crawl away when Renn whispered frantically at him. ‘Captain, do they have aegises?’ Cobbler rolled onto his back to face Renn. William, in a moment of near hysteria, almost burst out laughing as he was reminded of the nude muses of Kamenetz that he had so often painted.


‘Remoras haven’t seen any, but that doesn’t mean they’re not there. Now shut up and stop hiding in the shade.’ The captain crawled away.


William smiled as Renn, grumbling, took up position beside him and hoisted his rifle. ’What I’d do to be stationed in Dorume. They’re probably watching all of this from the tip of the World Spine, commenting on how fucking droll it would be to be serving in Syspirro.’


‘Perhaps a tour in Dorume would have been less impressive for Mother?’


A laugh strangled before it could carry across the glade. ‘Fuck you, William.’ The half-hour spent in the mud and sweat was sluggish in its passing.


A radio nearby brrrped. ‘They’re coming.’ Tamper said softly, rolling his shoulder and adjusting his grip. He adjusted the sights on his autorifle.


William squinted and looked forward. After moments of seemingly complete stillness, he saw the enemy advance.


The Calmodians barely made a sound as they moved through the forest. Their steps were the rustle of leaves as they darted from trunk to trunk, their mottled uniforms blending in seamlessly with their surroundings; they were only visible when they moved. There was no steady drumbeat of marching feet, nor the bellows of command. There was only the wind and the distant symphony of war.


William’s brow furrowed as the Calmodians approached. He had expected something different, something unusual with the appearance of their foe, but he had also expected them to be fundamentally similar in uniform and equipment. They were not. As they drew nearer, his artist’s eye caught the detail of the cowled invaders. The Calmodians approached, nimble despite their thick armour and heavy tabards and cloth faulds. Their helmets were smooth and matte, save for the bug-eyed lenses that glinted glossy and black as the sun peeked through the canopy. Their uniforms, which must have been hellishly hot in Syspirro’s clime, were camouflaged soil to the naked eye. Gloved hands that reached to the elbow, like a smelter’s mittens, clutched long-barrelled rifles, the magazines under the receivers large and round like bulging metal guts.


William paused, closed one eye, and aimed down his own rifle. They were reaching the edge of the glade now.


The Calmodians became wary as they came to the clearing. They hesitated. Some moved up the hill but did not go far.


There was no cover to be had if they were to cross, but the glade was but one of many that pocked Hannipt’s rainforest. If they wanted to push through to Lunonen, they would have to cross the clearings.


Other battles still raged in the north, west, and southwest, though their tumult had diminished in William’s ears. The armies of the Fourth and Sixth were surrounded in Hannipt as the Calmodian forces had enveloped from the north and marched from Portlock. Fort Lunonen was their only redoubt in this hellish forest, and there would be no retreat. William’s heart pounded.


Wordlessly, the Calmodians emerged from the forest. They were great in number. They moved forward in a loose row of lines, spread out from one another.


A rumbling in the distance. ‘Aegis,’ Tamper said quietly. Renn cursed. William hissed at him to be quiet. The Calmodians were halfway across the glade now. They were hunched, ghoulish in their stature and alienness. On the right flank, more Calmodians emerged and began moving up the hill. Tamper cursed.


The rumbling grew louder, the bellow of combustion more prominent.


William licked his lips. The trigger felt slick.


A slate grey shape trundled behind the marching troops; trees were brushed aside and crushed, cracking and snapping loudly.


Two hundred feet from the Gildaynean line of the glade they came.


With a belch of black smoke, the aegis broke through the treeline, its treads revolving slowly as it lumbered forward. A group of infantry came in its wake, half-crouching as they hurried in a double column behind it.


The aegis had completely left the forest and was gaining speed when two more broke the treeline on either side. Then another three. Behind them, Calmodians increased their pace and rushed into the glade. William’s finger hovered over the trigger.


‘Fire!’


As the order rippled down the line, the Gildaynean bellow pipes sounded, their calls resounding through the field.


William pulled the trigger, the rifle bucking back into his shoulder and rattling the bones in his hands. A Calmodian crumpled and fell, his carcass hidden by the grass.


The clearing erupted into a kaleidoscope of light as tracers flew.


The Calmodians nearest to the Gildaynean line fell like shorn wheat and did not rise again. Behind, Calmodians threw themselves to the ground and, lying prone, returned fire. The Calmodians did not use bolt-action rifles; more akin to Tamper’s autorifle, Cal’s standard-issue armament spat rapidly into the Gildaynean lines, shredding earth and flesh.


The foremost Calmodian aegis shuddered to a halt while its accompaniment took cover behind it. They poked their heads out from behind the metal beast and fired.


One Calmodian was unlucky as a Gildaynean bullet smashed through his bugeye lens and exploded out the back of his head, shattering the helmet into splinters.


William fired repeatedly, ducking his head behind the shattering col root as he drew the bolt or took fire. Renn worked wordlessly to his left, his movements mechanical as he ducked, reloaded, fired.


Spiralling contrails shot across the glade as three Gildaynean squads fired their impaler rockets into the nearest aegis. Two of the explosives hit at the wrong angle and ricocheted off into the treetops. The third scored a solid hit and erupted, though it did nothing but scorch the aegis’s hull.


The aegis’s cannon, as if in response, swivelled in smooth motions and methodically fired down the Gildaynean line.


The coaxial autoguns of the Calmodian aegises caused more havoc than their cannons. Three nozzles on each side of the machines fired into the forest, ripping through tree trunks and foxhole dirt, sailing through Gildaynean bodies in bloody bursts.


More Calmodians took position among the trees on the other side of the clearing. William could hear the screams of the dying as if their lungs were being emptied right into his ears. The stench of blood and fuel and earth filled his nostrils.


Grenades were lobbed through the air by both sides, mutilating rather than outright killing more often than not, if the howls were any indication.


The aegises swivelled and fired again and again, creating craters in the line. One hit the col tree beside William.


The tree groaned and fractured, falling away from him. Splinters flew, scratching and bloodying his face, but he kept firing. He glanced as he reloaded; Renn was alright, his face bloodied and grim as he reloaded his own rifle. Screams seeped from the collapsed tree as more Gildayneans surged forward to fill in the gap.


The aegises shot down the line, fire eructing from their muzzles like clockwork. Many Gildayneans fled before the machines’ wrath, fleeing to the rear positions.


A great eruption sounded from behind William. He didn’t turn as the Gildaynean aegises, positioned some distance behind the frontline, began firing on the Camodian war machines. William, who was no merchant nor curator of war, even he could tell the difference in quality between the metal beasts of the two nations. The Gildaynean aegises, the Snapperheads, they were much slower to fire, and their armaments were less accurate. From what William could see, the Calmodian aegises were larger and faster, manoeuvring about the battlefield with an agility he didn’t believe possible for the vehicles.


As another encroaching Calmodian fell in front of him, something tugged at his arm, skewing his aim as he fired. He turned and saw that Renn was pulling him back from the ruined col tree.


Tamper was shouting, but it took a moment for William to process what he was saying.


‘The hill! They’re taking the hill!’


Renn pulled him again. He was surprisingly strong.


‘We need to pin them down for the Syspirron!’ Tamper bellowed as they moved through the trees. Brine Company and half of Atoll Company bounded down the shallow trenches into the forest as Tamper led the way.


A thought latched to William’s thoughts like a symbiotic growth. I have killed. He had killed at least three men; he had seen the pink mist surge from them. I have killed. He followed Tamper.


The men of Atoll and Brine, one hundred and sixty of them, dove behind sandbags strewn about the shelled remains of an outpost placed at the hill’s crease. A meagre concrete fortification had been erected, its walls marred with small black pits.


A handful of soldiers were already shooting at the advancing Calmodians; they came in throngs through the woods, darting up the hill. Snug in a cleft between two sandbags, a private manned an autocannon and sprayed into the broiling mass before him, its barrel running hot before automatic fire from Cal ripped through his chest.


Renn and William took position together behind the sandbags. Beside them, Robert and Gregory manned the autocannon. Robert wrested the dead private off the gun as Gregory replaced the ammo belt. Amon stood, frozen and out of cover, before Lou Baker grabbed him by the collar and yanked him to the ground.


‘You’re right, Mother would find this much more impressive!’ Renn shouted at William, a smiling snarl on his face as he aimed over the sandbags. A bullet ricocheted off his steel helmet and he sprang back down. Others were not so lucky.


A grenade, sailing in a smooth arc over the barricade, thudded into soft earth between the men of First Squad. Lou, standing in front of Amon who was prostrate and curled before the barricade, promptly threw it back.


‘Where the fuck are those bark-eaters?’ Captain Cobbler screamed. He stood, bent over like an old man as he made to shift between cover, perhaps to gain a better vantage of the hillock before him, when a bullet caught him in the throat.


William, his back to the spilling sand of the punctured bags, peered over the barricade as men cowered down beside him. The Calmodians were almost on them. He saw them running up the hill, where the Syspirron should be gathered. He lowered his head and, breathing evenly, he reloaded his rifle. He was almost out of ammunition.


Where are the Syrpirron?


A small, yellow object landed a few feet in front of him. It exploded.


William felt as if the sun had suddenly materialised before him. He felt heat on his face and saw only white, searing white. A brace of bells rang inside his ears in a bid to crack his skull open. He fell to the ground and writhed in pain, or at least he thought he did, as he could perceive nothing but that pain.


Seconds or hours passed, and he began again to perceive shapes. He blinked hard. He was lying on his back, facing the barricade. His ears still rang, and the sounds of battle were dampened, but he took no notice.


His attention was consumed by the Calmodian standing on the sandbags.


Stark and terrible against the skyline, the man seemed inhumanly tall, his otherness accentuated by the bulging glossy eyes set in his helmet.


Wiping blood from his eyes, William could see that the Calmodian was holding a shotgun.


A figure lurched toward the Calmodian. Renn. Or Amon?


The Calmodian raised his weapon and shot from the hip, the muzzle flash reflected momentarily in his helmet’s eyes. The figure fell.


William frantically scrambled backward, clawing for his rifle. Figures groaned in the dirt beside him.


The Calmodian swivelled on his hips and levelled the shotgun at William.


William closed his eyes. Half a dozen, rapid heartbeats passed.


The gunshot did not come.


A deafening torrent of voices in a foreign language swept over him; the Calmodians were going to overrun them.


William opened his burning eyes. The Calmodian was still standing on the barricade, but he was looking up the hill and pointing with a thickly gloved hand.


Though his ears still rang, he could hear the Calmodian shouting through his strange helmet in a lilting voice that he could not understand.


A cataclysmic chorus of foreign tongues rang through the glade.


William, not daring to hope, groped for his rifle, or any rifle that might have lain in the mud.


Though he could not understand in that moment, the cacophony of foreign voices that were reaching a crescendo were not Calmodian.


They were Syspirron.


A mottled green blur sped past the Calmodian.


William watched, fascinated, as the soldier’s head simply disappeared; a spurt of blood squirted from the cleaved neck. The body fell backward and landed into the bed of soft soil with a soft thud. The head fell between William’s legs.


Inhuman screams tore through the forest, sending shivers up William’s spine like no artillery salvo could. Crawling back to the fortifications, he looked through a gap that had been blasted in the sandbags. He hadn’t believed the stories about the Syspirron, and he couldn’t believe them now.


A battalion of Syspirron riders coursed down the hillock and bore down upon the Calmodian flank, a ceaseless wave crashing upon a crumbling rock. The Syspirron were resplendent in crimson silks, azure silks, ribbons trailing from the horns that tipped their battle visors, masks that depicted grinning, nightmarish visages. Though all the Syspirron riders carried small carbines on their backs, most wielded long and curbed sabres that fell and rose as they tore among their quarry, dragging arced tapers of blood into the air. The Syspirron screamed like demons as they hacked and thrust about themselves with a terrible and beautiful ferocity as they sheared through Calmodian helmets and skulls.


William was awestruck, but not by the riders. His mouth hung agape as he watched their steeds rip into the Calmodian infantry.


The snarling and scaled simurs, taller than the largest warhorse, loped down the hill on all fours, pouring into the glade, decimating the Calmodians caught in the open. The simurs darted amongst the surprised soldiers with astonishing speed. Their large, triangular heads lunged forward, savagely biting into flesh and latching on like a limpet, wrenched back and forth until the Calmodians between their jaws went limp. William, even behind the barricade, could hear the bones being ripped out of sockets as the great lizards rent flesh from bone. The simurs lashed out with muscular forearms and kicked with powerful hindlegs, mutilating with ease. Blood spilled like fountain water before the beasts. The odd armour that the Calmodians wore did nothing to stop the onslaught; the emerald nightmares swung their scythe-tipped tails with savage abandon.


The Calmodians fired at the oncoming horrors, aiming at the simurs rather than the riders. Most hit their mark, but they did not fell the lizards. Rather, the bullets seemed only to enrage the beasts.


A single Calmodian, his helm marked with a red chevron, stood his ground and aimed his rifle carefully. He fired. The bullet caught a Syspriron rider centre mass and unseated him. The riderless simur, enraged, devoured the distance to the Calmodian and disembowelled him.


The aegises in the glade had turned to shoot into the approaching cavalry.


Twisting gun nozzles spluttered into the crowd of Syspirron. The aegis closest to the reptilian flood fired its main cannon twice, atomizing a cluster of riders and their mounts. As a brace of simurs fell, bifurcated by the blast yet still lashing out at any Calmodians fool enough to approach, more poured into the glade.


A trio of simurs and their riders darted to the flank of the nearest aegis. The simurs, their heads bowed low, rammed into the aegis in unison, lifting it off its treads. With incomprehensible strength, the simurs pushed and pushed, their long forearms supporting the motion as they toppled the aegis. As the war machine rolled onto its back, the three Syspirron riders jumped onto its belly; after a moment, a hatch in the machine’s underside opened. Two of the Syspirron, their carbines unslung, fired into the hatch. The third dropped a grenade inside. They jumped back onto their steeds and re-joined the fray. The grenade went off, and blood and shrapnel erupted from the aegis’s gut.


A resounding cheer rose from the Gildayneans down the line as the unending Syspirron stream filled the glade.


William staggered to his feet and surveyed his surroundings. The forest floor was littered with bodies. A few men moved about the barricade, searching for survivors. The fingers of the decapitated Calmodian spasmed in front of him. About to turn away, William paused. From the Calmodians helmeted head ran a tube, cleanly cut by the simur’s tail. The other end of the tube ran into a large metal tank attached to the Calmodian’s back. Picking up his rifle, the thought niggled at him. He couldn’t explain why.


William stumbled to where Renn had fallen. The body was face down. William knelt, grimacing as he rolled the corpse over. It wasn’t Renn, but a soldier that he did not recognise.


‘Renn?’ William called, cupping his hands about his mouth to diminish the din, though it did nothing. ‘Renn?’ Beside him, Robert and Gregory moved sluggishly, clutching their heads and rubbing their eyes. Lou dragged Amon under the arms, the young man’s face slack and pale; the Fourth Army veteran took the moment of reprieve to drag Amon behind the front line.


William, ignoring the rest of his squad, turned over another body, another unknown private who had had taken a bullet to the heart. He rested a bloody hand on the sandbags and took a moment to breathe. Where the fuck did he go?


‘Tend not the dead, soldier. Hannipt will care for them.’


William looked up and recoiled.


An amber simur eye scrutinised him over the barricade with a curious intelligence. Fetid breath puffed from a slit nostril as the beast proudly reared its cerulean crown. Three horns, connected by black webbing, protruded from the simur’s head. A gangly Syspirron sat lazily in the saddle, his black mask depicting a laughing, broken-toothed face. A fine sabre of smoked steel hung from his hip and red leather reins dangled loosely in his hands. A dozen other riders waited nearby; they watched William intently, their carbines at the ready.


The rider with the laughing mask sighed. ‘You Gildayneans let us do all the work, then you will take all the credit. Bah! Typical.’


His accent was thick, his hidden mouth moving around the Gildan words as if he were rolling marbles in his mouth.


An explosion sounded nearby. William looked west. The Calmodians were being pushed back from the east, though they remained stubborn in the face of the envelopment. Laughing Mask said something in Syspirron to his fellows. They barked, their laughter harsh and out of place as the fighting continued. William’s gaze turned back to the Syspirron before him; oil-black eyes glistened behind the mask, staring keenly at him, as if waiting.


William, moving slowly, picked up a rifle; it lay in the mud like a proffered sword. The riders on the other side of the barricade bristled. He raised a hand placatingly and turned, intent to search for the Renn.


A shout came from behind him. William turned to see the Syspirron riders moving their simur toward him. The man with the broken mask nonchalantly raised his hand and the riders stopped immediately.


‘They want to kill you, Gildaynean,’ Laughing Mask said. Could a smile be heard in those words?


‘Why would that be?’ William said, trying to sound braver than he felt as his voice cracked like he had just been thrust into pubescence. ‘We’re all on the same side, aren’t we?’


‘Same side, yes, this is knowledge known,’ Laughing Mask said. ‘But manners lacked are a grievous insult here in Syspirro. Would you turn your back on your King Austrum before he gave his dismissal?’


‘Syspirro has no king,’ William said, irritated at the lax nature of this Syspirron.


Laughing Mask shifted in his saddle and leaned down, the simur beneath him shaking its head vigorously and yawning as it did so, pink membrane stretching over jagged teeth.


As Laughing Mask moved, William could see his cloth tunic was emblazoned with an emblem: a scarlet diamond bisected by three azure lines. The Syspirron royal crest.


‘No,’ Laughing Mask said, whispering as if to confide in William, ‘but it does have a prince, and your life was sheltered by his hand.’ The man pulled off his mask, revealing a sallow face beneath, a face that William had seen plastered everywhere in the streets of Portlock; the face of Prince Mura of Syspirro.


William paled as he realised his mistake and quickly bowed his head. Mura laughed.


‘That is more like it, fish-fucker.’ He reclined in the saddle and patted the simur on its thick neck.


A twig snapped and the prince’s guards stood in their saddles; the simurs growled, their intonations ending in low hisses. Mura, a bored expression on his face, looked over William’s shoulder. ‘Approach,’ he said. Sergeant Tamper stepped in beside William. He bowed deeply, his arms rigid at his sides, as was the Syspirron way.


‘Prince Mura. It is an honour,’ Tamper said.


‘I would imagine so. To us you have something to say?’


‘Your Majesty, I don’t know what information you have but our scouts report that the Calmodians aren’t retreating. They’re digging in at the western edge of the clearing.’


‘What of it, soldier? My riders are eating up their flank. It matters not that they dig their heels to the ground.’


‘We have intel that Calmodian aegises are retreating from the forest’s edge, they’re not moving into to support their ground troops. Surely there is a reason—’


Mura waved his hand dismissively. ‘They know they cannot best simur riders, this is apparent to all.’


‘Your Majesty, perhaps it is best to err on the side of caution—’


‘Begone from me, Gildaynean. Return to your shoals. I will not flee with a tail tucked, not while Syspirro cries.’


Mura turned from them and kicked the horned simur in the ribs. The creature emitted a reverberating bellow and ambled down the glade, gaining speed as it melted into the ever-flowing river of Syspirron riders, the prince’s guard following closely behind. Tamper wasted no time in contemplating Mura’s decision.


‘Atoll! Brine! On me! We’re re-positioning to that hill,’ Tamper bellowed. He didn’t wait for an answer and began sprinting. The companies followed. As William ran, Renn stumbled into step beside him. He grinned at William, his face streaked with blood.


Great gouges scarred the soil and trees were knocked askew where the simur had charged. Some of the great beasts had been felled by a torrent of Calmodian bullets, along with their riders; the Syspirron who had survived their steeds knelt by the simur corpses, weeping.


A collar of rock, some thirty feet high, jutted from the northern face of the hill. Reaching the crest, the companies set about bolstering the position. General Kerghast had ordered that only a small contingent of Gildayneans occupy the hilltop before the engagement so as not to draw Cal’s attention; they had all been slaughtered by the Calmodians, who had in turn been slaughtered by the Syspirron.


The hilltop had no constructed fortifications save for a meagre trench system that ran along the summit’s circumference; its lines of barbed wire had been trampled by the Syspirron. Tamper ordered the companies to erect rifle shields along the top of the trench, to place sandbags and fill the gaps in the defence. They obeyed. There were two storerooms in the trench where they found the required materials, as well as two ‘25 Wertmire repeater cannons; they were promptly deployed on the cusp of the trench.


‘William! Renn!’ The two privates handed the ammunition belts they were carrying from the storeroom to a pale private from Atoll; they could hear the munitions jingling on the youth’s neck as they sped to their sergeant at the fore of the trench. Tamper stood before a portable radio pack. ‘William, you know how to use this,’ he said. ‘Herwyn is dead.’ Herywn was Brine Company’s radio operator. William nodded and hoisted the radio onto his back.


‘I want the two of you to climb that rock face,’ Tamper said, gesturing towards the sharp outcrop to the north. ‘I want vantage on any counterattacks. Renn, you go with him and provide him cover. If you spot Cal coming, return immediately.’


‘Won’t the Syspirron take care of them?’ Renn asked.


Tamper laughed harshly. ‘The Syspirron will plunge where the fighting is thickest; the fighting will not be thickest here, but I’ll be damned if I give the Calmodians a site of elevation. Hop to it.’


William and Renn set off, running past the scrabbling men of the companies and out of the trench.


The rock was not far away from the trench. It rose up from the side of the hill like smeared paint scraped and dried to stone, its tip narrow and jagged. William and Renn approached the eastern side of the outcrop where there was a steep but manageable incline. It brought them unnervingly close to the hill’s edge and a sheer cliff with a precipitous drop. William, momentarily forgetting himself, could not help but stare at the sight of Hannipt below.


Before him, colossal smokestacks stained the skyline. Lower Hannipt sprawled as far as the eye could see, a sea of emerald treetops peppered with devastating blazes. Blinding flashes, great and small, twinkled in the depths of the forest to the northeast, where the Sixth guarded the northern passage to Lunonen. William’s breath caught in his throat; countless fires burned as Calmodian aircraft ruled the skies, flying in quartets like whirling hawks as they descended upon the pockets of Gildayneans spread throughout Hannipt.


The sun was setting, its bloodred shades fusing with the black brush strokes of smoke, but no man who fought in Hannipt that day was cognisant of it.


He looked over the precipice. Far below, through the leaves of cols that hung from the cliff, he could see the Demerero flowing. It was a sheer drop; the simurs were excellent climbers it seemed.


‘William.’ Renn, dark rings under his eyes where there none that morning, was looking at him oddly. ‘Are you alright?’


William smiled. ‘Yes.’


Renn nodded, his expression uncertain. William began climbing the rock face. The rock’s surface was composed of porous, cutting limestone. He moved carefully as he searched for purchase. The radio felt heavy on his back and sweat poured in torrents through his uniform. Eventually, about halfway up, he reached a small flat on the rock’s western side. William carefully unloaded the radio and pulled out a small but powerful pair of field binoculars.


He removed the mouthpiece from the radio pack. ‘Tamper? DeLat reporting, acknowledge. Over.’


Tamper’s distorted voice sounded immediately. ‘Acknowledge. Report on north of the glade. Over.’


Peering over the northern slope of the rock, he held up the binoculars and scanned. He spoke into the mouthpiece. ‘There are some Syspirron driving the Calmodians back on their lines; they have them on the run. Over.’


‘What about the west? Over.’


The sun sank further into the horizon.


William swung the binoculars to his left and peered down into the glade. From his perch, he witnessed little dioramas of the battle. Five miles away a large outcrop of Calmodians fought along the western perimeters of the glade. Six Calmodian aegises blasted into the Syspirron, though the machines themselves seemed to be retreating deeper into the depths of Hannipt.


The Syspirron riders had split into two surging groups, both flowing into the trees on either side of the resisting Calmodians. Muzzle flashes in the trees demarked more entrenched Calmodians deeper in the northern woods.


Masses of Gildayneans rushed from the south to the frontline, making their way toward the firefight.


He swept the binoculars back to Cal’s line of defence and frowned. They had stopped firing and were throwing themselves to ground. To surrender so easily after all of that?


The sun had almost disappeared now, its dying light steeping the sky in gold.


William held the mouthpiece to his mouth. ‘Calmodians are dug in at the western edge of the glade. Several platoons to the northwest. Syprirron are taking the flanks. Aegises are retreating from—’


The Calmodian missile plunged into the earthen heart of Hannipt Forest.


The missile had rocketed from a Calmodian stratobomber thirty seconds earlier, breaking the sound barrier as it hurtled towards the battleground. It struck the locus of the Syspirron surge. A dark cloud of forest soil swelled upwards hundreds of feet into the air. The brute concussive force of the initial shock was enough to vaporise beast and man where it landed.


Upon impact, the missile’s tip exploded, releasing its payload of liquid fire. In a fraction of a second, the blooming chemical cocktail, released from its cage, tasted the oxygen and spread rapidly in search of more.


In the blink of an eye the glade was immersed in hellfire. The Syspirron and Gildaynean soldiers drenched in it rolled futilely as they tried to quench the clinging flames, their cries drowned out by the expanding crimson maelstrom.


The piercing shrieks of the simurs were the only sounds to break the roar of sweeping flame. Blood boiled and skin turned to crackling as the wave of broiling red fury engulfed the forest. The Gildayneans fled from their defensive line, the whip of terror licking at their soles as they stampeded from the flames.


Blinded by the searing torrent, the immolated forces of the Syspirron and Gildayneans could not see the shadowed figures that now walked amongst them.


The masked, bug-eyed Calmodians had stepped into the glade. Their heavy armour, so laborious to wear in the close heat of Hannipt, now protected them entirely from the chemical fire that raged about them.


As the fire blossomed, the Calmodians walked through the burning glade, the long blades in their hands reflecting the dancing flames. Most of the survivors, badly burnt and writhing, were found on the edges of the glade. With determined precision, the bug-eyed soldiers put a comforting hand over the eyes of their foes and sunk their knives between ribs into fading hearts.


The fire, hungering, leapt from blade to root to trunk, its greed pulling it into the forest. In moments, even the tallest trees beside the glade were doused in the crimson tide, their branches dripping scorching death.


The skies, as if furious at this terrible happening, roiled. Stratus gathered over the battlefield. The bed of Hannipt began to seethe from the encroaching fire, but something else stirred in the dark forest; starting as a fine mist, then gathering into fat droplets, rain began to fall upwards from the soil, slowly at first, then rising in a torrent.


The skies flashed as, for the first time in living memory, a snapstorm formed above the Syspirron mainland. The rains, conducted under thunderous direction, endured the fires and swept into the sky, the trees and flames whipping into a frenzy as the wind swept its wrath across Hannipt.


The Calmodian inferno was not doused, nor could it be, but the storm, this small miracle, kept the conflagration at bay, enveloping the glade and halting the fire’s advance.


To the dying, it did not matter.


The last man in that nameless glade fell.


The sun set.
